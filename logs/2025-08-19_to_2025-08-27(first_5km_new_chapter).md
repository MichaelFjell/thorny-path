**Prisoner squats** (hands interlaced behind your head, elbows pulled back) vs. **standard squats** (hands on chest or out front) add several biomechanical and postural demands—*not just cosmetic*:

---

### **What prisoner squats actually add:**

1. **Upper back and scapular activation**
   - **Pulling elbows back:** forces scapular retraction, firing rhomboids, mid/lower traps, and posterior delts.
   - **Prevents rounding:** can’t hunch or “chest collapse” at the bottom. Trains upright torso—critical for all loaded squat progressions.

2. **Core engagement**
   - **Hands behind head:** eliminates “counterbalance” from hands in front, so your abs/obliques have to work harder to stabilize your spine.
   - **Anti-extension training:** prevents lumbar over-arching, especially at the bottom.

3. **Neck and thoracic mobility**
   - **You must keep neck neutral** (not chin-forward), opening thoracic spine and improving posture—translates directly to better running and lifting form.

4. **Depth and form feedback**
   - **Cheating is harder:** with hands on chest you can “cheat” by folding forward or using arms for momentum. Prisoner position forces true squat mechanics—if you lean forward, you immediately feel neck tension or fall off balance.
   - **Great self-diagnostic:** if you can’t hit depth or keep elbows back, it exposes tightness in hips, thoracic spine, or shoulders.

---

### **Who benefits most?**
- Anyone with forward-head, kyphotic (“computer hunch”) posture—this squat undoes tech-neck.
- Runners and lifters needing better core and upright posture.
- Anyone who wants to make bodyweight squats *harder without adding load.*
